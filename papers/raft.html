In Search of an Understandable Consensus Algorithm

Diego Ongaro, John Ousterhout · 2014 · USENIX ATC

Read PDF → Raft Website →

Summary

Raft is a consensus algorithm designed to be more understandable than Paxos while providing equivalent fault tolerance and performance. The authors explicitly optimized for understandability, making it easier to teach and implement in real systems.

Key Ideas

Leader Election

Raft uses a strong leader model. Only one server acts as the leader at a time, and all client requests go through it. If the leader fails, followers can start an election to choose a new leader.

Log Replication

The leader receives commands from clients, appends them to its log, and replicates them to follower servers. Once a majority of servers have stored the entry, it's considered committed.

Safety

Raft guarantees that committed entries will never be lost and that servers will apply the same sequence of commands to their state machines.

Why It Matters

What makes Raft compelling isn't performance—it's clarity. The algorithm is decomposed into relatively independent subproblems: leader election, log replication, and safety. This makes it much easier to understand and implement correctly than Paxos.

Many production systems now use Raft, including etcd (used by Kubernetes), Consul, and CockroachDB. Its understandability has made it the go-to choice for distributed consensus.

My Notes

Reading this paper made distributed consensus click for me. The visualization on the Raft website is particularly helpful for understanding the election and replication process.

One interesting design choice: term numbers act as a logical clock, helping servers detect stale information. It's a simple but elegant solution to a complex problem.
